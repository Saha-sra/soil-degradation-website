Conservation measures involve a set of farming practices and techniques that help elevate the state of the degradation. These measures are often targeted with long-term exposure rather than for a short period. These also help the farmers boost their performances easily over time.

Resolution

Sustainable land management approaches, such as conservation agriculture, precision fertilization methods, afforestation, and pollution control measures, are essential for addressing the detrimental effects of all types of soil degradation before they become irreversible.

Soil Degradation is the loss of land production capacity in terms of loss of soil fertility, soil biodiversity, and degradation. Soil degradation causes include agricultural, industrial, and commercial pollution; loss of arable land due to urban expansion, overgrazing, and unsustainable agricultural practices; and long-term climatic changes. According to a recent report to the United Nations, almost one-third of the worlds farmable land has disappeared in the last four decades. It was also reported that all of the World topsoil could become unproductive within 60 years if current rates of loss continue.

Causes of Soil Degradation


Soil degradation can be caused by both natural and human factors. Natural causes of soil degradation include weathering, erosion, and nutrient depletion. For example, weathering can cause soil to become less fertile over time as nutrients are leached away by rainwater. Erosion can also contribute to soil degradation by removing the topsoil layer, which is typically the most fertile layer of soil. Nutrient depletion can occur when crops are grown without adequate fertilization or when soil is overgrazed by livestock. Human activities also contribute to soil degradation. Land use changes such as deforestation, intensive agriculture, and urbanization can cause soil degradation by increasing the amount of exposed soil, altering soil structure, and reducing vegetation cover. In addition, overgrazing, improper land management practices such as tillage, and the use of chemicals such as fertilizers and pesticides can contribute to soil degradation.


Consequences of Soil Degradation


Soil degradation can have a range of negative environmental and social impacts. Reduced soil productivity due to soil degradation can lead to reduced crop yields, decreased plant growth, and reduced biodiversity. In addition, soil degradation can lead to the contamination of waterways with sediment and agricultural chemicals, which can harm aquatic ecosystems and negatively impact human health.Soil degradation can also have economic impacts. For example, reduced crop yields due to soil degradation can lead to increased production costs for farmers due to the need for additional fertilizers and other inputs. In addition, soil degradation can lead to increased infrastructure costs due to the need for erosion control measures such as silt fences and sediment basins.


Solutions to Soil Degradation


There are a number of solutions to mitigate the impact of soil degradation. One of the most effective solutions is to implement sustainable land management practices that promote soil health and reduce degradation. For example, conservation tillage, cover cropping, and agroforestry are all practices that can help protect the soil from degradation while also promoting soil health and fertility. Another solution is to promote the use of conservation measures such as terracing, contour farming, and riparian buffers. These measures can help reduce the velocity of water flow over the soil surface, reducing the amount of soil that is carried away by the flow of water. Reforestation efforts can also help to mitigate soil degradation. Trees help to stabilize the soil, reduce water runoff, and provide a habitat for wildlife. In addition, reforestation can help to sequester carbon, mitigating the impacts of climate change. In urban areas, green infrastructure such as rain gardens, bioswales, and green roofs can help to reduce soil degradation by absorbing stormwater runoff and reducing the velocity of water flow. Education and outreach can also play a role in mitigating soil degradation. By educating landowners and farmers about the impacts of soil degradation and the benefits of sustainable land management practices, we can promote the adoption of practices that help protect the soil and reduce degradation.

Practice Details
Contour ploughing plowing along the contours of a field to create terraces, especially on 2-10% (gentle) slopes1 slows down water streams, which can otherwise erode soil or dig trenches into it practiced historically in the UK, still practiced in places such as Coon Creek Watershed in Wisconsin (USA) or Grenada (Spain).
Crop rotation growing different crops in a field in different years. keeps the soil healthy by preventing nutrient depletion practiced historically by Greeks, Romans, etc. and still in use in rural areas of Italy, Greece, Romania, the Philippines, etc.
Cover crops planted between cash crops (the main crops that are grown for sale). plants fit as cover crops include the velvet bean Mucuna, Desmodium grasses, Aeschynomene plants, etc. helps to improve soil health by adding organic matter and preventing erosion used in areas of the world such as West Africa
